You have the product, the data stack, and live subscribers. What you do not have is a system connecting them. That is the gap.

The challenge is upstream. ASO and paid acquisition are being treated as two separate problems. They are not. Every keyword you rank for shapes the audience that responds to your ads. The Plan
Three pillars, ninety days, one operator
01

ASO rebuild

The App Store is a search engine. Start treating it like one.

  • Full keyword audit targeting 2-2-3, DuPont, Pitman, rotating shift, night shift, and profession-specific terms for nurses, dispatchers, and plant workers
  • Screenshot redesign brief so the schedule-to-alarm value is legible in under three seconds on the store listing
  • Product Page Optimization A/B test framework, ready to run inside App Store Connect from week two
  • Competitor gap map across the top 8 ranked shift worker scheduling apps currently taking your keyword traffic
02

Meta architecture

A $1,000 budget works when the structure is right.

  • Audience segmentation by profession: nurses, plant workers, dispatchers, first responders, each split by shift pattern type
  • Three creative angles tested in week one: schedule confusion, missed alarm fear, and pay tracking relief
  • Campaign event schema wired directly to RevenueCat trial start and subscription purchase events
  • Weekly creative rotation so the budget does not burn against fatigued audiences before the data has spoken
03

Subscription conversion

The install is not the win. The second billing cycle is.

  • PostHog session replay review to identify exactly where trial users drop off before reaching the paywall
  • Onboarding sequence audit with targeted interventions at the 48-hour and day-7 drop-off windows
  • Paywall copy and pricing frame test: annual vs monthly vs weekly, with a clear winner criteria before month two
  • RevenueCat cohort tagging by acquisition source so we know precisely which channel produces subscribers who stay
04
Their Customer
Who the funnel is built for
Demographics

Shift workers, 25 to 45, on iOS, running rotating or non-standard schedules across healthcare, manufacturing, and emergency services.

The highest-value segment is healthcare shift workers: nurses, CNAs, and paramedics. Plant and manufacturing workers are the second tier. Both have tight word-of-mouth networks within their profession and very low tolerance for apps that feel built for someone else.

Psychographics

They do not want a productivity app. They want to stop missing shifts and misreading their own schedule.

This is not a GTD or self-optimisation crowd. The pain is physical and immediate: rotation patterns that change weekly, alarms that fire at the wrong time, pay calculations scratched on a notepad at the end of a 12-hour shift. The app has to feel reliable, not clever.

Decision drivers

A review from their own profession converts harder than any star rating.

A 4.8-star average means nothing without a review from a nurse on a 3-12 rotation or a dispatcher working DuPont. Profession-specific social proof and schedule-type screenshots in the store listing outperform generic claims at every stage of the funnel.

Platforms

App Store for discovery, Facebook groups for word of mouth, in-app for the conversion.

The path is almost always: App Store keyword search or Meta ad, store listing, install, trial, paid. Facebook groups for nurses, first responders, and plant workers are where organic referrals compound. Both channels feed the same funnel.
